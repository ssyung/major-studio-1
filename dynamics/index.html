Renewable Energy and Good Governance in Africa

Introduction

The resource curse, also known as the paradox of plenty, refers to the paradox that countries with an abundance of natural resources tend to have less economic growth, less democracy, and worse development outcomes than countries with fewer natural resources.

Does the "resource curse" apply to renewable energy?

Visually pairing World Governance Indicators with renewable energy potential, the emerging pattern interplay leads to further lines of inquiry:

  • Do countries with weak World Governance Indicators and high renewable energy potential have a higher susceptability to the resource curse?
  • Are countries with strong World Governance Indicators and high renewable energy potential strong candidates for renewable energy pilot projects?

Legend

Each country element of the visualization maps several data points:

  • Each of the six axes maps one dimension of the World Governance Indicators1, in units ranging from -2.5 to 2.5: Voice and Accountability, Political Stability, Government Effectiveness, Regulatory Quality, Rule of Law, and Control of Corruption
  • The yellow circle maps estimated renewable energy potential2, in terawatts
  • The yellow circle maps reported renewable energy installed capacity3, in megawatts

Africa's renewable energy potential is vast (1 terawatt = 1,000,000 megawatts) - mapping this in comparison with installed capacity has visual limits. In order to visualize this relationship, the renewable energy potential area is scaled to be 4x the area of the installed capacity.

Sudan, Somalia and Democratic Republic of Congo share relatively low World Governance Indicator scores and high renewable energy potential. Sudan and Congo have modest renewable energy capacity, while Somalia has none. Comparing the individual indicator axes, Somalia is weaker than Sudan and Democratic Republic of Congo in Rule of Law, Regulatory Quality and Government Effectiveness. In all three countries, Political Stability ranks very low. All three countries have a pronounced indicator "jaggedness" - scores across the six axes fluctuate and distort the resulting shape when the axes are connected.

South Africa, Namibia and Botswana all rank strongly across most of the World Governance Indicators and all have a relative indicator evenness. All three countries have fairly high renewable energy potential, but only South Africa has a meaningul installed renewable energy capacity. No immediate relationships are evident between the individual indicators and lack of renewable energy capacity in Namibia and Botswana, but these countries may prove to be strong candidates for renewable energy projects.
